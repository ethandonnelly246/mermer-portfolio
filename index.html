About Ethan
Donnelly

My design work is about disrupting a comfortable way of thinking. Graphic design is a space where culture splits and identity refuses to stay still. It's about pulling apart the visual languages we are given and rebuilding them into something that demands to be felt.

My practice is derived from typography, photography, and mixed media. I treat each element not as a tool but as raw material. Everything should be cut, warped, layered, and contextualised. All typefaces carry history. Photos hold stories. My job is to break both apart and find what's underneath.

The result is work that doesn't sit quietly on a page, it punches through the silence. Culture and identity do not sit passively in my work. They are the tension that drives it. I'm drawn to the questions that don't resolve easily: who gets to define visual culture, whose story gets stylised, and what happens when those systems are deliberately interrupted. These aren't obscure concerns. They appear in every creative decision I make from the weight of a letterform to the grain of a repurposed image.

I work based on instinct whilst maintaining a rigorous process and attention to detail. I gather, clash and refine obsessively. I believe honest design comes from friction, between the expected and the unsettling, the polished and the raw.

I am still shaping my voice as a designer, but certain of its direction: provocative, human, and unapologetically grounded in the complexity of the world I'm designing for.

01 Nineteen Eighty-Four Mixed Media / Book + Poster +
Exploration — Concept + Medium

1984 is a book I've read several times. Orwell's vision of the future is scarily real and that feeling needed to be on the cover. Early experimentation pushed texture, medium, and colour — red carried through everything because it had to. Blood, danger, warning, revolution. The design was always going to live in that territory.

Mixed Media — Adobe Photoshop, Illustrator
First sketches — concept thumbnails

Seven directions explored — from the Buddha-like authority figure to the all-seeing eye with rays, Big Brother portraits, the Groupthink surveillance composite, and the broadcast tower as state control. The red two-tone and grey palette locked in early across all of them.

Rendered directions — before the final

Three distinct poster directions before the book cover. "Not Your Brother" used grey tonal layers with a spray-paint eye — confrontational but held back. The red face with repeated quotation marks below felt more mechanical, state-issued. "Groupthink" went fully raw — white chalk marks on red, surveillance eye and fragmented figure. Each one pushed the red further until it became the only possible ground.

Nineteen Eighty-Four

A cover you have to keep looking at to see everything in it. The background writing, the scribbles, the eye, George Orwell's name discretely tucked into the right side. It's filthy and chaotic because that's the world Winston Smith actually lives in, regardless of what the Ministry of Truth wants you to think. Built to bridge generations — the rebels and the ones who remember.

Front + back cover + spine — print ready artwork
08.06.49 — Watch

The poster is the cover evolved for the street. Same concept, inverted — background red now, everything jumps out and creates fear and mania. Removing "1984" from the top was the right call. The date it was published sits there instead, pulling people in and making them wonder. A rebellion poster doesn't announce itself. It makes you look it up.

A2 Poster — Adobe Photoshop, Illustrator
02 Billy's Brew — Endless Summer Packaging / Label Design +
Exploration — Concept + Direction

Who doesn't want an endless summer? The brief was simple — design a beer label that feels like a red hot day in Noosa drinking with your mates. Early concepts pushed different directions before landing on flat 2D illustration with a psychedelic edge. The colour has to be vibrant, the character had to be slightly unhinged, and the whole thing had to make you laugh.

Packaging Design — Adobe Illustrator
Inspiration — reference material

The reference pool was deliberately wide — risograph gig posters, bold character-driven illustration, two-colour screenprint work. The common thread was handmade energy and irreverence. Nothing polished. Nothing corporate.

Initial directions — early digital explorations

Four early digital directions — a decorative red/orange label concept, a gestural red-on-white abstraction, organic pink forms, and an early character sketch. The ornate and painterly directions gave way to the flat, bold illustration approach that became the final. Simplification was the right call.

Endless Summer IPA

60s and 70s art as a reference point, given a contemporary punch. The character illustration captures that feeling of a big summer day, where you're sunburnt, slightly delirious, and completely content. Colour was everything here. It had to pop off the shelf and pull you in before you'd even read the name.

Front + back label — print ready artwork — Adobe Illustrator
Billy's Brew — Noosa

Seeing it on the bottle confirmed the decisions. The die-cut, the vibrant palette, the comedic character — it all holds up at scale. Pre-press setup included outlines, separations, die-cut layers, and 3mm bleed ready for print production.

03 Riverland Dark Sky Reserve Brand Identity / Capstone +
Exploration — Research + Moodboard

The Riverland Dark Sky Reserve name represents conservation and research, combining the natural environment with the science world. The early moodboard and sketches started to form that identity, slowly forming itself into the digital renders. This is where the earth's rich colour palette started to take hold.

Brand Identity — Adobe Illustrator
Moodboard — research imagery

Milky Way skies, winding outback roads, red ochre country, the Murray River at dusk. The moodboard established the tonal territory before a single mark was made. Thirteen logo concepts were sketched — stars, river forms, terrain layers, Milky Way spirals, the "21.9" coordinate, and multiple wordmark directions including RMIDSR as an abbreviation.

Riverland Dark Sky Reserve

The Riverland Dark Sky represents the land and the sky and the connection between the two. The natural colours communicate more than any intricate logo could. They needed room to breathe and that's the treatment they got. The brief required renaming the reserve entirely — River Murray International Dark Sky Reserve became Riverland Dark Sky Reserve, a name built for the consumer, not the committee.

Config Variable Extra Bold — Thermal VF — Adobe Illustrator
The Placement

When put in place this logo draws your eyes in and tells you exactly what you need to know without having to try.

04 Green Room — Morning Breeze Packaging Design +
Exploration — Concept + Direction

The toothpaste market is a cookie cutter industry and it was time for that to change. The brief was to redesign packaging from the ground up and bring something fresh to a shelf full of the same formula. Early sketches and research pushed toward an environmentally conscious brand built for people who care about the little things and want to do their part for the bigger picture.

Packaging Design — Adobe Illustrator
3D form explorations — structure before colour

Form was solved before colour. Three structural 3D renders established the packaging geometry — tube, box, and hybrid form options — all in greyscale so the shape decisions weren't compromised by palette. The third exploration introduced a cyan highlight edge, the first hint of where the colour story was heading.

Colour iterations — finding the combination

Twelve colour combinations tested across the same form. Deep teal, red, mint, sage green, forest green, brown — the matrix was exhaustive. The chosen combination (type CMYK 76, 44, 82, 42 on background CMYK 35, 13, 34, 0) landed on the right side of soothing without going clinical.

Green Room — Morning Breeze

Vibrant yet soothing, fun yet purposeful. The die-cut wave creates negative space and depth that no flat box on the market is doing. 100% recycled paper packaging because if a small brand can do it right, maybe the big names will follow. The retro surf vibe modernised — the finalised design speaks for itself.

Front, back + side panel — print ready artwork — Adobe Illustrator
Built for the Shelf

Thinking in three dimensions was the biggest challenge and the biggest reward. Seeing it constructed confirmed every decision. The wave, the colour, the character of it — it holds up from every angle.

Physical construct + digital mockup — Adobe Illustrator
05 The Godfather of Swiss Punk Editorial / Typography +
Exploration — Colour + Structure

This project was about embodying a philosophy, not just referencing one. Early sketches pushed grid structures and typographic hierarchy until the palette became the defining decision — four directions tested before the right one just felt correct.

Typography / Editorial — Adobe InDesign, Illustrator
Initial sketches — layout and grid structure

Four structural thumbnails established the column grid, fold lines, image placement, and typographic hierarchy before anything was built in InDesign. Direction 4 — with the X-axis crossing and diagonal tension — is where the Weingart methodology started to show up in the layout itself.

Colour palette — four directions tested

Black/grey/orange, black/grey/black, orange/grey/orange, navy/burgundy — four palette combinations tested at tint scale. The orange and cool grey landed because it felt like something Weingart himself would reach for: utilitarian, warm, and slightly confrontational. Sempletone+ 172 C and Cool Gray 5 C became the defining combination.

Behind the Spread

Built with rigidity before it was broken. The grid came first — columns, baseline, hierarchy locked in — because you can't subvert a system you haven't built. That's the Weingart methodology and it's mine too.

The Godfather of Swiss Punk

Orange and grey came purely from vibe — putting myself in Weingart's shoes and feeling what he'd reach for. This project is a projection of my practice. I love bright fun design but this is where I work differently. The details are in the simplicity done right.

Two-page editorial spread — Adobe InDesign
